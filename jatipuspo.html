Jasminum spp.

The genus Jasminum belongs to the family Oleaceae and is widely distributed, with the majority of species found in India, China, and Malaysia. With about 200 species, it is collectively known as jasmine. Flowers and leaves of jasmine plants have been used in folk medicine to treat various ailments such as abdominal pain, asthma, fever, and toothache. In Asian traditional medicine, jasmine is recommended for liver complaints, dysentery, and skin diseases.

Modern herbal medicine has incorporated traditional knowledge, resulting in innovative treatments. Several species of Jasminum from Manipur, with potential therapeutic properties, are listed below.

Jasminum sambac Ait (Thaballei)

Jasminum sambac is highly valued for its medicinal properties. In Manipur, it is known as Thaballei and is extensively cultivated. The flowers are commonly used in traditional medicine and tea industries. In folklore practices, the juice of Thaballei flowers or roots is recommended for diabetic patients. Phytochemical studies have revealed various compounds present in the flowers, including coumarins, cardiac glycosides, and essential oils. Extracts from J. sambac have shown antifungal activity against Malassezia species isolated from human skin.

Jasminum multiflorum Burm.f. (Local name; Kundo)

Jasminum multiflorum, known locally as Kundo, is used in folk medicine to treat piles. It is cultivated for religious purposes and is characterized by white, sweetly scented flowers. The boiled extract of Kundo flowers is orally administered to treat piles.

Jasminum nitidum Skan (Local name; Warakundo)

Jasminum nitidum, locally known as Warakundo or Angelwing Jasmine, is used in Manipur's folklore system to treat various conditions such as diabetes, headaches, and cough. Ethnobotanical studies have highlighted the importance of these aromatic plants, emphasizing the need for conservation efforts.

Jasminum auriculatum (Ayurvedic name; Yuuthikaa)

Jasminum auriculatum, cultivated throughout India, is used as a stomachic in traditional healing practices. Scientific studies are needed to validate the efficacy of traditional plant-based remedies.
