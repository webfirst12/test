Magnolia grandiflora L.

Magnolia grandiflora L.: Exploring Traditional and Modern Applications

Introduction

Genus Magnolia, consisting of approximately 210 species and belonging to the family Magnoliaceae, is native to tropical and subtropical regions of the Americas and Southeast Asia. Among its species, Magnolia grandiflora stands out for its ornamental value, adorned with large, fragrant flowers.

In traditional Chinese medicine, the bark of Magnolia officinalis is widely utilized and known as 'Houpo'. This bark is incorporated into various formulations, offering a range of therapeutic benefits.

Ethnobotany of U-thambal

Magnolia grandiflora, referred to as U-thambal in the Manipuri language, holds cultural significance in the region. Its roots, flowers, leaves, and bark find applications in traditional healing practices, ranging from treating kidney stones to malaria.

The bark of U-thambal is utilized in various medicinal formulations, reflecting its diverse pharmacological properties. Additionally, its flowers serve as natural shampoos, contributing to traditional hair care practices.

Genus Magnolia and Phytochemicals

Over 250 chemical compounds have been identified in various parts of Magnolia plants. The bark, particularly of M. officinalis, contains significant phenolic compounds such as magnolol and honokiol, renowned for their therapeutic effects.

Clinical Evidences

Traditional uses of Magnolia bark align with modern pharmacological investigations. Its applications range from asthma treatment to gastrointestinal health and diabetes management. Magnolol and honokiol exhibit diverse pharmacological activities, including anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and neuroprotective effects.
